Why Orion Needs Uprighting Bags — and BLOON Does Not

NASA’s Orion crew module requires a dedicated Crew Module Uprighting System (CMUS) with 5 helium-inflated bags to recover from an inverted “Stable 2” orientation. 47% of Apollo ocean splashdowns ended inverted. The BLOON capsule’s toroidal geometry eliminates this problem entirely.

Shape Comparison: Frustum vs Torus

NASA Orion (Frustum / Cone)

A 57.5° truncated cone — wide heat-shield base (5.02 m), narrow apex top (2.0 m). This asymmetric shape creates two stable floating orientations:

  • Stable 1 (upright): heat shield down, apex up. Wide base provides large waterplane → strong GM.
  • Stable 2 (inverted): apex down, heat shield up. Narrower waterplane, but geometry still permits a marginally stable equilibrium with crew hatch submerged.

Result: 5 CMUS bags (5000 psi helium COPVs) required on every mission. Uprighting takes up to 4 minutes. System failed partially on EFT-1 (2014) — only 2 of 5 bags held pressure.

BLOON (Torus / Donut)

A symmetric toroidal shell with conical keel. The ring geometry produces a single stable floating orientation (upright):

  • Waterplane area is large and roughly similar at any heel angle (annular cross-section).
  • The keel cone (35.9 kg at Z = −0.7 m) lowers CG well below the centre of buoyancy.
  • There is no second stable equilibrium — the inverted position is confirmed unstable (GM < 0 when inverted).

Result: No uprighting system needed. Self-rights from 90° in ~16 s. Airbag (already present for landing) provides 3.8× stability bonus.

The Fundamental Difference

🔎
A cone has two ends — a torus has none

Orion’s frustum shape means it can sit stably on either the wide base (heat shield) or the narrow apex. Both orientations trap water below a convex hull, creating a positive metacentric height. A torus has continuous rotational symmetry about the vertical axis and only one flotation equilibrium because the waterplane second moment of area is always dominated by the large ring diameter, overwhelming any CG offset. The keel further enforces this single equilibrium by concentrating mass at the lowest point.

🚀
Orion’s CMUS adds 5 single-point failure paths. BLOON needs zero.

Each CMUS bag requires a helium COPV, pyrotechnic valve, inflation plumbing, and fabric bladder — all of which must survive reentry heating, parachute deployment shock, and ocean splashdown impact. The EFT-1 partial failure (3 of 5 bags failed) demonstrates the difficulty. The BLOON capsule’s inherent single-stable-point geometry eliminates this entire subsystem, saving mass and removing failure modes.

Historical Context: Apollo Uprighting

The Apollo Command Module (CM) had the same two-stable-point problem. Of 19 ocean splashdowns, 9 (47%) ended in Stable 2 (inverted). Apollo used 3 inflatable bags (“uprighting balloons”) in the nose section. These were pressurised by the crew manually activating compressors — a process that took several minutes with the astronauts hanging upside-down in their harnesses, often seasick. The Orion CMUS is the modern evolution of this system, now automatic but still mechanically complex.

Conclusions & Recommendations

Primary Conclusions

  1. Hydrostatically stable upright in all configurations. GM ranges +0.56 to +2.29 m.
  2. Airbag is transformative — 3–4× GM increase, excellent wave riding. Auto-deploy on water contact.
  3. Self-righting confirmed 0°–90° in dynamic simulation (16–18 s calm water).
  4. Inverted recovery is marginal. Asymmetric airbag inflation may be needed.
  5. 7° natural list from thermal/safety mass concentration. Recommend ballast redistribution.
